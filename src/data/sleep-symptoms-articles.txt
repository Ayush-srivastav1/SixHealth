<p>
Excessive daytime sleepiness (EDS)—persistent difficulty staying awake during waking hours—affects 10-20% of adults. More than simple tiredness, EDS compromises safety, productivity, and quality of life. Identifying the underlying cause enables targeted treatment.
</p>

<h2>What Distinguishes EDS from Fatigue</h2>
<p>
Daytime sleepiness involves actual sleep propensity—difficulty staying awake, falling asleep unintentionally, overwhelming drowsiness. Fatigue involves low energy and motivation without specific sleep tendency. People with EDS doze off in meetings, during conversations, or while driving; fatigue sufferers feel low-energy but stay awake. Sleep study testing (multiple sleep latency test) confirms EDS by quantifying sleep tendency—abnormally quick sleep onset confirms EDS. Important distinction because causes and treatments differ.
</p>

<h2>Primary Causes of EDS</h2>
<p>
Insufficient sleep duration—sleeping less than personally required—is the most common cause. Individual sleep needs vary 7-9 hours is average but some need 6, others need 10; chronically insufficient sleep causes EDS proportional to sleep debt. Sleep fragmentation from sleep disorders prevents restorative sleep—frequent arousals, breathing interruptions, or leg movements leaving total sleep time normal but sleep quality poor. Sleep apnea, restless leg syndrome, periodic limb movement disorder causing frequent arousals create EDS despite 8-hour sleep. Circadian rhythm misalignment from shift work, jet lag, or intrinsic circadian disorders like delayed sleep phase syndrome cause EDS. Narcolepsy, idiopathic hypersomnia (neurological EDS of unclear cause), or other neurological conditions create primary EDS.
</p>

<div style="text-align:center; margin-bottom:20px;">
  <img 
    src="https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1517286428247-d1cc0fb8c83c?auto=format&fit=crop&w=900&fm=webp&q=70"
    alt="Sleep improvement strategies"
    style="max-width:100%; border-radius:8px;"
  />
</div>

<h2>Secondary Causes</h2>
<p>
Medical conditions—hypothyroidism, anemia, autoimmune disease, chronic infections—drain energy. Depression and other mood disorders frequently cause EDS. Obesity worsens sleep apnea and contributes to general EDS. Medications—antihistamines, sedating antidepressants, muscle relaxants—cause EDS as side effect. Substance use including alcohol (which fragments sleep), cannabis, and stimulant withdrawal cause EDS. Chronic sleep restriction from demanding schedules perpetuates EDS.
</p>

<h2>Clinical Evaluation</h2>
<p>
Sleep history documents sleep duration, quality, and symptoms. Sleep diary tracks timing and sleepiness. Questions about driving difficulty, work impact, attention problems help quantify severity. Physical exam may reveal obesity, anatomy predisposing to sleep apnea, thyroid abnormality, or other medical causes. Sleep study (polysomnography) evaluates for obstructive sleep apnea, restless leg syndrome, and other sleep disorders. Multiple sleep latency test quantifies EDS objectively. Blood tests screen for thyroid dysfunction, anemia, infection, or metabolic disorders.
</p>

<h2>Treatment Approaches</h2>
<p>
Increasing sleep duration if insufficient is obvious first step—even 1-2 hours additional sleep transforms EDS in sleep-deprived individuals. Treating underlying sleep disorders (sleep apnea with CPAP, restless leg syndrome with medication) resolves EDS. Addressing circadian misalignment—shifting schedule or using light therapy—helps circadian disorders. Treating underlying medical or psychiatric conditions helps. Stimulant medications (modafinil, amphetamines) improve alertness in narcolepsy and other primary conditions. Behavioral modifications—avoiding driving when sleepy, strategic napping, caffeine use timing—support management.
</p>

<p>
Excessive daytime sleepiness is treatable once underlying causes are identified.
