<p>
Most psoriasis cases are diagnosed through clinical examination alone, based on characteristic appearance and location of skin lesions. Dermatologists develop expertise recognizing psoriasis's distinctive features, enabling accurate diagnosis in most cases without additional testing. Understanding how diagnosis occurs helps patients recognize when to seek evaluation and what to expect.
</p>

<h2>Clinical Examination</h2>
<p>
Dermatologists visually examine affected skin, evaluating lesion appearance, distribution, and characteristics. They look for well-demarcated red plaques with silvery-white scales, the hallmark psoriasis features. The Auspitz sign—pinpoint bleeding when scales are scraped away—is characteristic of psoriasis though not specific. The Koebner phenomenon—development of new lesions at sites of skin trauma—suggests psoriasis when present.
</p>

<h2>Medical History Importance</h2>
<p>
A thorough medical history helps establish diagnosis. Dermatologists inquire about symptom onset, flare patterns, triggering factors, family history of psoriasis, and impact on daily life. Previous treatments and their effectiveness guide current management. Systemic symptoms suggest possible psoriatic arthritis or other complications. Emotional distress from psoriasis affects treatment planning.
</p>

<h2>Key Diagnostic Considerations</h2>
<p>
Lesion distribution—elbows, knees, scalp, lower back—favors psoriasis diagnosis. Symmetrical involvement of both sides of the body is characteristic. Nail involvement (pitting, discoloration) supports psoriasis diagnosis. Associated itching or burning but lack of primary vesicles distinguishes psoriasis from conditions like epidermolysis bullosa. Long disease duration—often years—indicates chronic inflammatory disease consistent with psoriasis rather than acute dermatitis.
</p>

  <img 
    src="https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1576091160650-112173f31c77?auto=format&fit=crop&w=900&fm=webp&q=70"
    alt="Specialized dermatological assessment"
    style="max-width:100%; border-radius:8px;"
  />
</div>

<h2>When Biopsy is Needed</h2>
<p>
Skin biopsy for histopathology confirms diagnosis when clinical features are atypical or uncertain. Biopsy is also performed if diagnosis changes management decisions or when multiple conditions are considered. Biopsy is usually unnecessary when clinical diagnosis is clear.
</p>

<h2>Differential Diagnosis</h2>
<p>
Conditions resembling psoriasis include seborrheic dermatitis, contact dermatitis, pityriasis rubra pilaris, and lymphomas. Systemic sclerosis and other connective tissue diseases occasionally mimic psoriasis. Fungal infections, particularly on palms and soles, may resemble psoriasis. Distinguishing features help dermatologists differentiate these conditions.
</p>

<h2>What to Tell Your Dermatologist</h2>
<p>
Provide detailed descriptions of symptom onset, changes over time, and triggering factors. Describe location and appearance of initial lesions and how disease has progressed. Discuss family history and personal medical conditions. Mention all medications and supplements taken. Describe impact on daily life, emotional effects, and previous treatments.
</p>

<p>
Open communication and thorough examination help dermatologists confidently diagnose psoriasis and develop effective treatment plans.

<p>

<p>

Different biopsy methods (punch biopsy being most common) may be used depending on clinical circumstances.

When both parents have psoriasis, risk increases to 50-60%. Genetic susceptibility combined with environmental triggers determines whether psoriasis develops.
</p>

<h2>HLA Genetic Association</h2>
<p>
Psoriasis is strongly associated with specific human leukocyte antigen (HLA) genes, particularly HLA-Cw6. HLA genes control immune system function, and specific variants predispose to psoriasis development. However, HLA testing is not routinely performed as presence doesn't guarantee disease development—approximately 10% of the general population carries HLA-Cw6 without developing psoriasis.
</p>

<h2>Multiple Gene Involvement</h2>
<p>
Psoriasis is a polygenic disease involving numerous genes, each contributing small effects. Over 60 genetic risk loci have been identified. Genes involved in immune regulation, particularly T cell function, are most relevant. Genes affecting skin barrier function, keratinocyte differentiation, and inflammatory pathways also contribute. The interaction of multiple genetic variants combined with environmental triggers determines disease development.
</p>

<h2>Environmental Triggers and Gene-Environment Interaction</h2>
<p>
Genetic predisposition alone doesn't guarantee psoriasis development. Environmental factors including infections (particularly streptococcal pharyngitis), stress, smoking, excessive alcohol, skin trauma, and certain medications trigger psoriasis in genetically susceptible individuals. The same genetic profile may manifest as psoriasis in one individual experiencing triggers while remaining dormant in another without trigger exposure.
</p>

<h2>Family Screening and Prevention</h2>

<h2>Disease Severity and Genetics</h2>

Genetic factors also influence disease severity and type. Family members may develop similar or different psoriasis manifestations. Psoriasis severity varies dramatically among patients from minimal localized plaques to extensive disease covering most of the body. Accurate severity assessment guides treatment decisions—mild psoriasis responds to topical treatments while moderate to severe disease often requires systemic therapy. </p>

<h2>Area of Involvement</h2>
<p>
Body Surface Area (BSA) measurement quantifies percentage of body affected by psoriasis. Mild psoriasis affects less than 10% BSA. Moderate disease involves 10-30% BSA. Severe disease covers more than 30% BSA. BSA calculation uses the "rule of nines"—palm equals approximately 1% BSA, enabling clinicians to estimate affected area. Extensive scalp or face involvement counts as more severe even if widespread body involvement is absent.
</p>

<h2>PASI Score</h2>
<p>
The Psoriasis Area and Severity Index (PASI) combines extent of involvement with severity of plaques, resulting in a numerical score from 0 to 72. PASI 0-7 indicates mild disease. PASI 7-12 represents moderate disease. PASI above 12 indicates severe disease. PASI scores guide treatment choices—different medications are approved for different severity levels. Regular PASI scoring monitors treatment effectiveness.
</p>

<h2>PGA Scale</h2>
<p>
The Physician's Global Assessment (PGA) provides overall disease severity rating on a 0-5 scale (clear to severe). PGA 0-1 indicates minimal disease. PGA 2-3 represents moderate disease. PGA 4-5 indicates severe disease. PGA is rapid assessment often used in routine practice.
</p>

<p>
Psoriasis patients have 1.5-3 times higher cardiovascular disease risk. Inflammation characteristic of psoriasis accelerates atherosclerosis. Dyslipidemia, hypertension, and diabetes are more common. <p>
Metabolic syndrome—combination of obesity, hypertension, dyslipidemia, and glucose intolerance—occurs in 30-50% of psoriasis patients. Depression affects 10-20% of psoriasis patients, significantly elevated versus general population. Anxiety disorders are similarly increased. Suicidal ideation has been documented in severe cases.
