<h2>Nausea and Vomiting</h2>
<p>
Nausea is one of the most feared side effects, though modern anti-nausea medications prevent it effectively in most patients. Preventive medications taken before chemotherapy are more effective than treating nausea after it develops. Eating small frequent meals, avoiding strong food odors, and staying hydrated help. Ginger, peppermint tea, and acupressure bands offer additional relief. Report severe nausea to your medical team so medications can be adjusted.

<h2>Hair Loss</h2>
<p>
Hair loss (alopecia) occurs with many chemotherapy drugs and is temporary, with regrowth beginning 3-6 months after treatment ends. Scalp cooling systems constrict blood vessels in the scalp, reducing drug delivery and can prevent or minimize hair loss in up to 60% of patients. Wigs, scarves, hats, and head wraps provide coverage. Many cancer centers have wig programs and free resources. Hair loss is emotional; support from loved ones and support groups helps.
</p>

<h2>Fatigue</h2>
<p>
Cancer-related fatigue differs from normal tiredness and can be profound, affecting mood and function. Gentle exercise like walking for 20-30 minutes daily actually improves energy levels. Adequate sleep, limiting alcohol, and nutritious meals support energy. Identify times of day when energy is highest and schedule important activities then. Herbal supplements and meditation may help some patients. If fatigue becomes severe, discuss with your team as it may indicate other conditions requiring treatment.

<h2>Mouth Sores and Taste Changes</h2>
<p>
Chemotherapy can damage mucous membranes, causing mouth sores (mucositis) and altering taste perception. Rinse mouth with salt water or baking soda solution frequently. Use soft toothbrush and avoid harsh mouthwashes. Sucking on ice chips or popsicles before and during chemotherapy minimizes mouth sores. Eat cool, soft foods and avoid sharp, acidic, or extremely hot foods. Taste changes typically resolve after treatment; in the interim, experiment with different foods to find what tastes acceptable.
</p>

<h2>Skin Reactions</h2>
<p>
Radiodermatitis (skin irritation from radiation) resembles a sunburn and requires gentle care. Use unscented lotions, avoid perfumed products and tight clothing over treated areas. Do not use heat, ice, or adhesive tape on the affected skin. Expose the area to air when possible. Avoid sun exposure during treatment and use high SPF sunscreen afterward. For chemotherapy-related skin reactions, keep skin clean and moisturized. Report severe reactions as prescription creams may help.
</p>

<h2>Low Blood Counts</h2>
<p>
Chemotherapy can lower white blood cells, red blood cells, and platelets. Low white counts increase infection risk; practice good hand hygiene and avoid crowded places during vulnerable periods. Red cell decline causes anemia and fatigue; nutritious diet with iron and B vitamins helps. Low platelets increase bleeding risk; avoid contact sports and report any unusual bruising or bleeding. Growth factors can boost white cell production. Your medical team monitors blood counts and manages complications.
