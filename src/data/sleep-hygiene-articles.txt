<p>
Sleep hygiene—practices and habits supporting healthy sleep—forms foundation of good sleep. While sleep hygiene alone insufficient treating established sleep disorders, it's essential component of any sleep improvement plan.
</p>

<h2>Consistent Sleep Schedule</h2>
<p>
Same bedtime and wake time daily—including weekends—anchors circadian rhythm powerfully. Rhythm regulated this way stabilizes sleep. Even 1-2 hour weekend variation disrupts circadian regulation. Choosing schedule fitting daily requirements important; schedule consistency matters more than exact time. Most people feel best with 6.5-8 hours sleep fit to their schedule. Consistent schedule usually improves sleep within 1-2 weeks and continues benefit indefinitely.
</p>

<h2>Caffeine Timing</h2>
<p>
Caffeine half-life approximately 5-6 hours—half remaining in body 5-6 hours after intake. Caffeine after 2 PM typically interferes with 11 PM bedtime sleep. Sensitive people need earlier cutoff. Coffee, tea, cola, energy drinks, chocolate contain caffeine. Gradual reduction prevents withdrawal headaches if dependent. Eliminating caffeine entirely often dramatically improves sleep in sensitive people. Some people tolerate morning caffeine well; others must avoid entirely. Individual sensitivity varies substantially.
</p>

<h2>Alcohol Timing and Limitation</h2>
<p>
Alcohol sedates initially but fragments sleep severely within 3-4 hours of intake. Drinking evening alcohol results in early night sleep followed by fragmented second half. Nightly alcohol damages sleep quality cumulatively. Regular drinkers develop tolerance; larger quantities needed for initial sedation. Sleep quality worsens significantly with regular alcohol. Red line: alcohol within 3-5 hours of bedtime disrupts sleep. Earlier drinking (1-2 hours) provides time for absorption/metabolism. Moderation (women ≤1, men ≤2 drinks daily) and avoiding before bed supports sleep.
</p>

<h2>Exercise Timing</h2>
<p>
Regular exercise (150+ minutes weekly) substantially improves sleep quality, reduces insomnia, improves daytime sleepiness. Aerobic exercise particularly beneficial. Timing matters—late-day strenuous exercise can delay sleep (4+ hours post-exercise elevated metabolic rate still). Morning and afternoon exercise optimal for evening sleep. Gentle evening activities (stretching, yoga) support sleep. Avoid vigorous exercise 3-4 hours before bed. If only evening exercise option, keep intensity moderate, complete several hours before bed.
</p>

<h2>Screen and Blue Light Avoidance</h2>
<p>
Blue light from smartphones, tablets, computers suppresses melatonin when exposed evening. Even dim screen use inhibits melatonin. Devices should be off or put away 30-60 minutes before bed. If screen use necessary before bed, blue-light blocking glasses (moderately effective) or apps reducing blue light (less effective than avoiding altogether) help. Reading physical books or relaxing activities preferred. Bedroom completely screen-free optimal. Morning/daytime screen use has no sleep impact; evening is problematic.
</p>

<h2>Bedtime Routine</h2>
<p>
Consistent wind-down routine (30-60 minutes before bed) signals sleep time to brain. Routine personal preference but typically includes: warm bath/shower, gentle stretching, reading, meditation, or journal writing. Consistency matters—same routine nightly trains body recognition. Dimmed lights, calming music, or aromatherapy enhance routine. Routine should involve little stimulation or work-related thought. Particularly important for people transitioning between work/high stress to sleep. Bed reserved for sleep not evening relaxation maximizes stimulus control.
</p>

<h2>Temperature Control</h2>
<p>
Cool bedroom (60-67°F) aligns with biological sleep need for lower core temperature during sleep. Hot bedrooms impair sleep. If room can't be cooled adequately, light bedding or fan helps. Conversely, cold extremities (cold hands/feet) are problematic for most—warm socks or mittens help. Comfortable thermal balance individually variable.
</p>

<h2>Limiting Naps</h2>
<p>
Brief naps (20-30 minutes) can refresh without impairing nighttime sleep. Longer naps or afternoon naps disrupt nighttime sleep particularly in people with insomnia. Sleep drive relevant—if nighttime sleep is good, brief nap OK; if fragmented nighttime sleep, eliminate naps. Naps after 3 PM impair evening sleep more than earlier naps.
</p>

<h2>Limit Liquids Evening</h2>
<p>
Excessive fluid intake evening leads to nighttime urination disrupting sleep. Limiting drinks 1-2 hours before bed reduces nighttime bathroom trips. Some urinary frequency occurs but excessive fluids worsen it. Particularly important for people with sleep maintenance insomnia or older adults.
</p>

<h2>Limiting Large Meals</h2>
<p>
Heavy, spicy, or acidic meals close to bed can cause discomfort, reflux, or delayed digestion interfering with sleep. Light snack 1-2 hours before bed if hungry. Bedroom hunger interferes with sleep but overfull stomach similarly problematic. Particularly important for GERD sufferers—lying flat with full stomach triggers reflux.
</p>

<h2>Creating Sleep-Conducive Bedroom</h2>
<p>
Dark, quiet, cool bedroom dedicated to sleep. Removing televisions, computers, work materials. Blackout curtains, eye mask if necessary. White noise if noisy environment. Quality mattress and pillows. Comfortable bedding. Pleasant but minimal décor. No bright clocks. Bedroom purely for sleep and intimacy.
</p>

<h2>Limiting Time in Bed Awake</h2>
<p>
If unable to fall asleep after 20-30 minutes, leave bed. Mental activity and worry build conditioning anxiety around bed. Breaking this pattern—getting up if can't sleep, returning when drowsy—is important stimulus control. Repeated sleep attempts create frustration and wakefulness. Removing this by leaving bed and relaxing elsewhere for a while, then returning when sleepy, prevents association between bed and wakefulness/frustration.
</p>

<p>
Consistent sleep hygiene practices provide foundation enabling restorative sleep quality.
