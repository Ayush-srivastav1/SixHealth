Psoriasis plaques and scaling are the hallmark features of psoriasis, the most visible and recognizable symptoms of this chronic skin condition. Understanding what these manifestations are, why they occur, and how to manage them is essential for anyone living with psoriasis.

<h2>What Are Psoriasis Plaques?</h2>
<p>
Psoriasis plaques are well-defined, raised patches of skin that appear red or pink with thick, silvery-white scales on the surface. These plaques can range from small lesions (less than 1 cm) to large areas covering several inches. They typically appear on the elbows, knees, scalp, lower back, and other areas of the body, though any skin surface can be affected.
</p>

<h2>The Scaling Process</h2>
<p>
The silvery-white scales result from rapid skin cell turnover. In psoriasis, skin cells mature and move to the skin surface seven times faster than normal, causing an accumulation of dead skin cells. When these cells reach the surface and shed, they create the characteristic dry, flaky scales. Scratching or attempting to remove scales can worsen lesions, leading to bleeding or secondary infection.
</p>

<h2>Common Characteristics</h2>
<p>
Psoriasis plaques have distinct features that differentiate them from other skin conditions. The borders are sharply demarcated, meaning there is a clear distinction between affected and unaffected skin. Plaques are typically dry and may be itchy or painful. The appearance can fluctuate based on season, stress levels, medications, and other triggers. Plaques may partially clear during warmer months (when sun exposure increases) and worsen during winter.
</p>

<h2>Managing Plaques and Scaling</h2>
<p>
Gentle care of affected skin helps minimize scaling and discomfort. Moisturizing regularly with thick creams or ointments helps reduce dryness and scaling. Avoid harsh soaps and hot water, which can further irritate skin. Topical treatments including corticosteroids, vitamin D analogs, and retinoids help reduce inflammation and scaling. Phototherapy using UVB or PUVA light slows skin cell turnover and may significantly improve plaques. In more severe cases, systemic medications or biologic therapies may be necessary.
</p>

<h2>When to Seek Professional Help</h2>
<p>
If plaques become increasingly extensive, painful, or resistant to treatment, consultation with a dermatologist is important. Signs of infection including increased warmth, pus, or fever warrant immediate medical attention. Plaques affecting joints combined with pain may indicate psoriatic arthritis, requiring rheumatology evaluation.
</p>

Understanding psoriasis plaques and scaling empowers you to work effectively with healthcare providers and manage your condition more successfully.

<h2>Types of Nail Changes</h2>
<p>
Nail pitting—small, punctate depressions in the nail surface—is the most characteristic finding. Pits range from subtle pinpoint depressions to larger, more visible holes. Oil-drop staining—bright yellow or orange patches under the nail—is highly specific for psoriasis. Onycholysis (nail separation from the nail bed) causes nails to lift and separate. <p>
Nail changes can cause significant functional difficulties. Separated or friable nails tear easily, affecting typing, writing, and other fine motor tasks. Nail changes may persist even when skin manifestations improve.

<p>
Acute pustular psoriasis or erythrodermic psoriasis can cause fever, sometimes reaching 104°F or higher. Fever indicates significant systemic inflammation requiring immediate medical attention. </p>

<h2>Fatigue and Energy Loss</h2>
<p>
Chronic inflammation characteristic of psoriasis causes persistent fatigue that affects daily functioning. Sleep disruption from itching and discomfort contributes to fatigue. Mental and emotional stress related to chronic disease increases exhaustion. Nutritional deficiencies from poor diet or malabsorption add to fatigue. Some patients describe feeling exhausted despite adequate sleep.
</p>

<h2>Associated Systemic Conditions</h2>
<p>
Psoriasis patients have elevated rates of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, obesity, and metabolic syndrome. Depression and anxiety occur in approximately 30% of psoriasis patients. Psoriatic arthritis affects joints in up to 30% of patients, causing pain, stiffness, and reduced mobility. Inflammatory bowel disease (Crohn's disease or ulcerative colitis) co-occurs in some patients.
