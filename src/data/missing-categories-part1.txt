<h2>Overview of Heart Disease</h2>
<p>
Cardiovascular disease encompasses multiple conditions affecting the heart and blood vessels, including coronary artery disease, heart failure, arrhythmias, and valvular disease. The most common type, coronary artery disease, develops when plaque accumulates in arteries supplying the heart, reducing blood flow. Other conditions may affect heart muscle function, electrical systems, or valve integrity. Each type requires specific assessment and treatment approaches tailored to individual circumstances.
</p>

<h2>Major Risk Factors</h2>
<p>
Key risk factors include high blood pressure, high cholesterol, smoking, diabetes, obesity, physical inactivity, and family history. Age and sex also influence risk, with men having higher risk at younger ages. Chronic stress and poor sleep increase cardiovascular strain. Understanding which risk factors apply to you enables targeted prevention efforts. Many risk factors are modifiable, meaning you can reduce them through lifestyle changes or medical treatment.
</p>

<h2>Prevention Through Lifestyle</h2>
<p>
Regular physical activity strengthens the heart and improves circulation. Aim for 150 minutes of moderate aerobic exercise weekly. A heart-healthy diet emphasizing vegetables, fruits, whole grains, fish, and limiting saturated fat reduces disease risk significantly. Maintaining healthy weight, limiting sodium and alcohol, and managing stress all protect cardiovascular health. Smoking cessation provides immediate benefits, with heart attack risk decreasing within hours of quitting and continuing to improve over years.
</p>

<h2>When to Seek Medical Care</h2>
<p>
Seek emergency care immediately for chest pain, shortness of breath, severe sweating, or arm/jaw pain, as these may indicate heart attack. Don't delay—call emergency services rather than driving yourself. For non-emergency concerns like fatigue, palpitations, or persistent shortness of breath, schedule evaluation with your healthcare provider. Regular checkups starting in your 40s (or earlier if risk factors present) enable early detection and treatment of cardiovascular disease, improving long-term outcomes significantly.
</p>

<h2>Understanding Menopause</h2>
<p>
Menopause is confirmed after 12 consecutive months without menstruation. The transition typically takes 4-10 years and includes perimenopause (variable periods with symptoms) and postmenopause (after the final period). Hormonal fluctuations during this time affect the brain, bones, heart, and metabolism. Not all women experience symptoms similarly; individual experiences vary widely based on genetics, lifestyle, and overall health. <h2>Overview of Parkinson's Disease</h2>
<p>
Parkinson's develops when nerve cells in the substantia nigra region of the brain gradually deteriorate, reducing dopamine production. Dopamine regulates smooth, coordinated muscle movement. Without adequate dopamine, voluntary movement becomes difficult. The disease progresses at different rates for different people; some experience slow progression over decades while others progress more rapidly. Genetic and environmental factors likely contribute to disease development, though the exact causes remain incompletely understood.

<h2>Early Symptoms and Diagnosis</h2>
<p>
Tremor at rest, particularly in hands, is the classic early symptom affecting many patients. Rigidity (muscle stiffness) and bradykinesia (slow movement) develop, making daily activities challenging. Postural instability and balance problems increase fall risk. Non-motor symptoms including fatigue, sleep disturbance, depression, and cognitive changes occur in many patients. Diagnosis relies on clinical evaluation by neurologists; no specific blood test confirms Parkinson's. Early recognition enables prompt treatment initiation, optimizing symptom management and quality of life.
</p>

<h2>Treatment and Long-Term Management</h2>
<p>
Levodopa remains the gold standard medication, converting to dopamine in the brain and effectively reducing motor symptoms. Other medications including dopamine agonists, MAO-B inhibitors, and COMT inhibitors offer additional options. Physical therapy, occupational therapy, and speech therapy address specific functional challenges. Deep brain stimulation surgically implanted in advanced cases provides significant symptom relief for selected patients. Regular neurologist follow-up, physical activity, cognitive engagement, and strong support systems all contribute to optimal outcomes.
