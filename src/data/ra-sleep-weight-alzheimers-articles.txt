<h2>Disease-Modifying Antirheumatic Drugs (DMARDs)</h2>
<p>
Methotrexate remains first-line DMARD, reducing inflammatory mediators and slowing disease progression effectively. Oral, subcutaneous, or IV administration options accommodate different patient preferences and tolerability. Regular monitoring ensures safety and efficacy. Sulfasalazine combines antibacterial and anti-inflammatory effects, useful particularly in seronegative RA. Leflunomide inhibits pyrimidine synthesis, reducing immune activation. Hydroxychloroquine provides modest benefit, often combined with other DMARDs. DMARDs require 6-12 weeks for effect manifestation, necessitating patience and compliance. Combination DMARD therapy optimizes benefit when monotherapy proves insufficient.

<h2>Biologic Therapies</h2>
<p>
TNF-alpha inhibitors (infliximab, etanercept, adalimumab, golimumab, certolizumab) block tumor necrosis factor, a central inflammatory mediator. IL-6 inhibitors (tocilizumab, sarilumab) address interleukin-6-mediated inflammation. Costimulation modulators (abatacept) interrupt T-cell activation. B-cell depleting agents (rituximab) reduce autoimmune B-cells producing destructive antibodies. JAK inhibitors (tofacitinib, baricitinib) target intracellular signaling. Biologic efficacy exceeds DMARDs alone, with 60-70% achieving low disease activity or remission. Combination therapy (biologic plus methotrexate) provides superior outcomes versus monotherapy. Biologic therapy requires careful patient selection and monitoring for serious infections and other complications.
</p>

<h2>Adjunctive Therapies</h2>
<p>
NSAIDs reduce pain and swelling, particularly useful early-disease while waiting DMARD onset. Corticosteroids in low doses reduce inflammation rapidly, though long-term use carries significant side effects limiting their acceptability. Combination therapy with DMARDs/biologics permits steroid tapering or discontinuation. Physical therapy maintains and improves joint function. Lifestyle modifications including stress reduction, exercise, and weight management support drug therapy. Smoking cessation improves DMARD efficacy and reduces cardiovascular risk. Sleep optimization supports immune function and pain management.
</p>

<h2>Cardiovascular Complications</h2>
<p>
RA patients have 1.5-2 times higher cardiovascular disease risk than general population. Chronic systemic inflammation accelerates atherosclerosis. RA itself is independent cardiovascular risk factor comparable to diabetes. Myocardial infarction and stroke occur more frequently and at younger ages in RA patients. Heart failure risk increases. <h2>Extra-Articular Manifestations</h2>
<p>
10-30% of RA patients develop extra-articular manifestations affecting lungs, eyes, skin, and other organs. Rheumatoid nodules appear as firm subcutaneous lumps. Interstitial lung disease causes progressive dyspnea and reduced oxygen; screening with pulmonary function tests and imaging identifies early disease. Scleritis causes eye inflammation and pain, potentially threatening vision. Vasculitis affecting skin and nerves causes rash and neuropathy. These manifestations indicate aggressive disease requiring intensified therapy. Biologic medications particularly benefit extra-articular manifestations.

Screening for and managing these comorbidities alongside RA treatment improves overall health outcomes. <h2>Obstructive Sleep Apnea</h2>
<p>
OSA involves repeated breathing pauses during sleep resulting from airway collapse despite respiratory effort. Oxygen levels drop with each pause, fragmenting sleep and stressing the heart. Risk factors include obesity, male sex, age, and anatomic factors. Symptoms include loud snoring, gasping arousals, morning headaches, daytime sleepiness, and potentially sudden cardiac death if untreated. Polysomnography confirms diagnosis, quantifying apnea severity. CPAP (continuous positive airway pressure) remains first-line therapy, delivering pressurized air maintaining airway patency. Other therapies include oral appliances, surgical procedures, and positional therapy. Weight loss in obese patients significantly improves OSA.
</p>

<h2>Insomnia</h2>
<p>
Insomnia involves persistent difficulty falling or staying asleep despite adequate opportunity, affecting 10-30% of adults. Acute insomnia follows stressors, resolving when stress resolves. Chronic insomnia persists beyond 3 months, often developing from behavioral factors including racing thoughts in bed, irregular sleep schedules, and poor sleep environment. Medical causes including sleep apnea, restless legs, medications, and medical conditions require identification and treatment. Cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia (CBT-I) provides durable improvement superior to sleep medications. Sleep hygiene optimization, stimulus control (bed reserved for sleep only), and sleep restriction (consolidating sleep) constitute CBT-I components. Medications provide short-term benefit but develop tolerance with long-term use.
</p>

<h2>Narcolepsy and Hypersomnia</h2>
<p>
Narcolepsy involves excessive daytime sleepiness with irresistible sleep attacks plus cataplexy (sudden loss of muscle tone triggered by emotion) in some cases. Caused by low hypocretin (orexin) levels regulating sleep-wake control. Diagnosis requires polysomnography plus multiple sleep latency testing (MSLT) demonstrating sleep attacks. Medications including stimulants (methylphenidate, amphetamines) address daytime sleepiness. Oxybate reduces cataplexy and improves nighttime sleep. Behavioral measures including scheduled naps and sleep hygiene support medication therapy. Idiopathic hypersomnia causes excessive daytime sleepiness without cataplexy. Similar treatment approaches apply.
</p>
