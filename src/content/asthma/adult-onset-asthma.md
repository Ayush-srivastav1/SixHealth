Asthma developing in adulthood presents unique challenges and characteristics distinct from childhood-onset disease.

## Why asthma develops in adults

Approximately 30-40% of adult asthma cases appear after age 40. Contributing factors include occupational exposures, severe respiratory infections, new allergic sensitization, hormonal changes, obesity, and environmental pollution.

## Differences from childhood asthma

Adult-onset asthma is more likely to be non-allergic and persistent. Symptoms can be subtle and attributed to aging or other comorbidities, delaying diagnosis.

## Diagnostic challenges in adults

Symptoms may develop gradually; spirometry and bronchoprovocation testing help. Consider CT and specialist referral when fixed obstruction or alternative diagnoses are possible.

## Occupational asthma and workplace assessment

Work-related exposures — chemical fumes, dusts, animal proteins — may cause or worsen adult asthma. Peak flow monitoring at work vs away and exposure assessment are useful diagnostic strategies.

## Management considerations

Treatment principles are similar to other asthma types but require attention to comorbidities, polypharmacy, and smoking cessation. Weight management and optimizing treatment for GERD or sleep apnea improve control.

## Prognosis

Adult-onset asthma often has lower remission rates but can be managed effectively with personalized therapy.
