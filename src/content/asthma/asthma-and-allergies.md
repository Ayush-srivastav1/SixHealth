The connection between asthma and allergies is profound, with allergic asthma representing the most common type affecting 80% of asthma patients. This article reviews mechanisms, common allergens, testing, avoidance strategies, immunotherapy, and the interplay between allergic rhinitis and lower airway disease.

## The Allergy–Asthma Link

Allergies and asthma share common underlying mechanisms involving immune system overreaction. When allergens contact the airway, IgE-mediated responses trigger mast cell degranulation, releasing histamine, leukotrienes, and other mediators that cause bronchoconstriction and inflammation.

## Major allergens

- Dust mites: ubiquitous indoor allergen present in bedding and upholstery.
- Pollen: seasonal exposures from trees, grasses, and weeds.
- Pet dander: cats and dogs release allergenic proteins.
- Mold: thrives in damp environments and can trigger severe reactions.
- Cockroach debris: a potent urban allergen.

## Testing and immunotherapy

Skin prick testing and specific IgE blood testing help identify triggers. Allergen immunotherapy (subcutaneous or sublingual) can reduce sensitivity over time and improve symptom control in selected patients.

## Allergen avoidance strategies

- Mattress and pillow encasings, hot washes for bedding, HEPA vacuums.
- Keep windows closed during high pollen seasons and use AC.
- Consider pet removal or strict pet-free zones for severe allergic asthma.

## Clinical implications

Treating allergic rhinitis alongside asthma improves overall control. Integrated care models that address both upper and lower airway inflammation yield the best outcomes.
